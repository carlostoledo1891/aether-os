# Caldeira Climate Patterns & Spring Health

## Climate Classification

Poços de Caldas sits within a **subtropical highland climate (Köppen Cwb)** at an elevation of approximately **1,200 m** above sea level. The alkaline complex forms a collapsed caldera structure ~33 km in diameter, creating a distinct microclimate influenced by altitude, topography, and the surrounding Serra da Mantiqueira.

## Precipitation Regime

- **Annual precipitation**: ~1,500 mm (range: 1,350–1,700 mm depending on year)
- **Wet season**: October–March, accounting for ~80% of annual rainfall
- **Dry season**: April–September, with June–August typically receiving < 30 mm/month
- **Peak rainfall month**: January (avg ~270 mm)
- **Driest month**: July (avg ~20 mm)

The wet season is driven by the South Atlantic Convergence Zone (SACZ), which channels tropical moisture across southeastern Brazil.

## Temperature

- **Annual mean**: 18–22°C
- **Warmest months**: January–February (mean max ~26°C)
- **Coolest months**: June–July (mean min ~8°C, occasional frost at higher elevations)
- **Diurnal range**: 10–14°C, typical of highland climates

## Spring Health & Hydrological Sensitivity

The Caldeira alkaline complex contains **1,092 catalogued springs**, making it one of the densest spring systems in Minas Gerais. Spring health is directly coupled to precipitation:

- **Recharge lag**: Springs respond to rainfall with a 30–90 day lag depending on aquifer depth and geology
- **Wet season**: Spring flow rates peak in February–April (lagging peak rainfall by ~2 months)
- **Dry season**: Flow rates decline progressively, reaching minima in September–October
- **Critical threshold**: When cumulative wet-season precipitation drops below ~1,100 mm, shallow springs show measurable flow reduction

## Historical Extreme Events

### Drought Events
- **2014**: Below-average rainfall (~1,150 mm) led to reduced spring flows across the eastern caldera rim. Several shallow monitoring wells showed 15–20% decline in water table.
- **2021**: Extended dry season (April–October) with below-normal precipitation affected tributaries feeding the Rio das Antas watershed.

### Flood/Heavy Rain Events
- **2020 (January)**: Extreme rainfall event (~180 mm in 48 hours) caused localized erosion and turbidity spikes in surface water monitoring points.
- **2023 (December–January)**: SACZ-driven heavy rains elevated sulfate concentrations in surface runoff near laterite exposures.

### Water Quality Impacts
- High-precipitation events increase surface runoff, which can mobilize naturally occurring sulfate and iron from the laterite/saprolite profile.
- Extended dry periods concentrate dissolved minerals in spring water, potentially pushing sulfate and conductivity readings upward.
- Both extremes require monitoring against CONAMA 357 Class 2 thresholds (sulfate < 250 mg/L, pH 6.0–9.0).

## Data Provenance

Historical climate data for the Caldeira site is sourced from **ECMWF ERA5 reanalysis** (1940–present) accessed via the Open-Meteo Archive API. Forecast data uses the **ECMWF IFS** model via Open-Meteo Forecast API (16-day horizon). All values represent the 0.25° grid cell containing the Caldeira site coordinates (-21.83°S, -46.56°W).
